Equipment Requirements

You need the following items on your computer to complete the course.

Oracle VM VirtualBox

VirtualBox runs on Windows, Linux, macOS, and it is a powerful tool to access and explore various operating systems.

Linux distribution

Following the physics department guidelines, students are recommended to purchase a specific raspberry pi 4 kit, but in this course, you are not required to have one. For the first part of the course, students are expected to have a Linux kernel installed to preform necessary tasks. I recommend to install Ubuntu Desktop on your virtual machine for user convenience.

Git and a Github account

we will discuss local and remote version control using Git. Therefore, you need the Git to be installed in your (Linux) comptuer. You will also need a Github account for remote synchronization.

Jupyter notebooks

In the remaining of the course, we will use Jupyter notebooks for completing problem sets. Conda environment has great supports on Python. I recommend Anaconda Distribution simplely because it runs on Windows, Linux, macOS.

Lectures, Discussion, and Office Hour

Lectures

Lectures are on Tuesday and Thursday from 11:00AM to 12:15 PM pacific time in Broida 5223. The instructor will introduce related backgrounds and concepts that are closely related to the problem sets.

Discussion Section

There are two discussion sections, where teaching assistants (TAs) will answer any course related concerns, including backgrounds, concepts, clarifications, etc.

Monday and Wednesday from 2:00PM-3:50PM, Broida 5223 (40717).

Tuesday and Thursday from 1:00PM-2:50PM, Broida 5223 (40725)

Office Hours

Office hour for both the instructor, Zihang Wang and TAs will be announced in the first lecture, or by appointments. The instructor is available for pre-scheduled meetings, as well as drop-in meetings, (Office location: Broida 6304).

Textbooks

The two textbooks are optional for your own reference,

The Linux Command Line, Fourth Internet Edition William E. Shotts, Jr.

A Student's Guide to Python for Physical Modeling: Updated Edition J.M Kinder and P. Nelson.

Problem Sets and Final Project

There will be approximately six (6) problem sets and one (1) final project. Late homework will not be accepted except at the discretion of the instructor. Most of the problem sets will be posted in the .ipynb format, and your lowest problem set score will be dropped. Please check instructor announcements frequently to avoid delays.

Final Exam

One pen and paper final exam will be given in the scheduled time,Wednesday, December 13, 2023 12:00 PM - 3:00 PM . You are allow to bring a single-page cheat sheet (any size), but it must be hand-written.

Grading

A letter grade will be assigned based on the following weighted average,

Five Problems Sets (after dropping the lowest score): 50%

Final Project: 30%

Final Exam: 20%

Course Policy and DSP Accommodations

Extensions and Plagiarism

Requests for extensions will be considered on an individual basis. Any code and related work MUST be written by the student who submits the work. You are encouraged to work with other students and explore generative AI models while working on problems, e.g. GPT, Co-pilot... However, you must type/write/explain the work in you own words.

DSP Accommodations

Reasonable accommodations will be granted to students with disabilities, and please inform the instructor via email by the end of the first week of instruction.

An Inclusive and Respectful Climate

Working and studying together is a crucial part of learning. The Physics Department wants everyone to feel welcomed and respected in class, in the lab, in study groups, and in the Physics Department building. If you find yourself avoiding department areas or in other ways curtailing your learning opportunities because of someone else’s behavior, please report the behavior to Physics Department administrators and/or a faculty member. We want to help.
